How to Play 3D Chess

8×8×8 is three-dimensional chess played on a cube of 512 squares — eight layers of 8×8 grids stacked vertically. Every piece from standard chess is here, but movement extends in all three dimensions. Rooks slide along 14 directions, bishops cut through space diagonals, and knights can leap to up to 24 squares. It's a completely different strategic experience from flat-board chess.

What Is 8×8×8?

The board is an 8×8×8 cube. Picture eight standard chessboards stacked on top of each other, where pieces can move between layers just as easily as they move across a single board. Each player starts with the standard set of pieces — king, queen, two rooks, two bishops, two knights, and eight pawns — all placed on the center layer (layer 4) in the familiar back-rank-plus-pawns arrangement. White moves first, and the goal is still checkmate. But with 512 squares and threats coming from 26 directions, every decision carries new weight.

Starting a Game

From the main menu, choose your mode. vs Computer lets you play against the AI at three difficulty levels (Easy, Medium, Hard) — start with Easy while you learn the board. Play Online connects you with other players around the world in real-time multiplayer — create a room or share an invite code with a friend. You can also pick game variants: Classic (standard setup), Barricade (extra pawns on adjacent layers), or Pawn Wall.

Board Controls

Rotate the board by clicking and dragging (or swiping on mobile). Zoom in and out with the scroll wheel or pinch gesture. Use the layer toggles to show or hide individual layers — this is essential for seeing pieces hidden behind other layers. Being comfortable with these controls is half the battle in 3D chess.

How Pieces Move in 3D

Every piece gains new movement options in three dimensions. Rooks move along any axis or planar diagonal (14 directions total). Bishops move along within-layer diagonals and space diagonals (12 directions). The queen and king can move in all 26 directions — the king one step at a time, the queen any distance. Knights make L-shaped jumps across any of the three planes (xy, xz, yz), giving them up to 24 possible moves. Pawns can advance forward, move up or down layers, or climb diagonally through layers, with up to 6 capture squares. There is no castling or en passant. Tips for Your First Game

Start on the center layer where all pieces begin. Don't rush to scatter across other layers — control the middle first, then expand to adjacent layers as you develop your pieces. Turn on threat highlighting to see which squares are under attack. This is invaluable when threats can arrive from 26 directions instead of 8. Use the layer toggles frequently to check what's happening above and below your pieces. Begin with the Easy AI to get comfortable with 3D movement before increasing the difficulty or going online.

Understanding Layers and Spatial Thinking

The biggest adjustment from 2D chess is learning to think vertically. A rook on the top layer can threaten pieces directly below it. A bishop can slice through space diagonals you didn't know existed. Pieces that look safe on one layer might be completely exposed from another. Practice by toggling layers on and off to build your spatial awareness. Over time, you'll start seeing the 8×8×8 cube as a single connected space rather than eight separate boards — and that's when 3D chess really clicks.
